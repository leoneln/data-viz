Course Overview > Course Introduction

Welcome to Data Visualization

Data Visualization is both an art and a science. It's the graphical representation of information, but it's also about transforming complex data into compelling narratives that capture attention and ignite action. This course delves into the power of data visualization to unlock insights from overwhelming datasets. You'll learn how to leverage the creativity of design principles alongside the accuracy of data analysis to craft effective visualizations that resonate with your audience.

This course equips you with the skills to transform raw data into clear, concise, and impactful stories. Through a blend of theory and hands-on application, you'll learn to uncover the stories hidden within your data sets.

Course Overview > Course Description

Course Description

Data Visualization creates value from complex data enabling insight, influence, and persuation. This course focuses on how to increase the likelihood of action based on insights from data by levering effective Data Visualizations techniques and best practices. The course includes a mix of theory and hands-on application using the latest data visualization technologies and techniques. Students will have a basic introduciton to Python, R, Power BI, and Tableau. Students will also learn about data visualization concepts such as preattentive attributes, and Gestalt principles of perception.

Course Overview > Learning Outcomes

Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of this course, students should be able to:

  • Articulate the interplay between art and science in creating effective data visualizations. Understand how visual design principles enhance data accuracy and clarity, fostering compelling storytelling.
  • Utilize contemporary data visualization tools to design and develop impactful narratives with data. This includes applying design principles to create aesthetically pleasing and informative visualizations.
  • Craft data visualizations tailored for different audiences. Consider the scientific principles of clear communication to ensure visualizations are readily understood and actionable.
  • Effectively present "stories with data" to a variety of audiences.

The Art & Science of Data Visualization

A Discussion Guide: Balancing Beauty and Insight

Does "beautiful" always mean "insightful"? This field lives at the intersection of two powerful disciplines: the rigorous Science of Truth and the expressive Art of Storytelling.

Art & Science > The Science

The Science: The Foundation of Truth

The "science" is the non-negotiable foundation. It dictates accuracy and comprehension. Get this wrong, and the visualization fails, no matter how beautiful.

DON'T: Misleading

This chart distorts perception. The Y-axis doesn't start at 0, making 'A' look tiny.

DO: Accurate

This bar chart correctly shows the true relationship. The Y-axis starts at 0.

Discussion: As future business leaders and information system professionals, how critical is the perceived objectivity of data visualization? Discuss the ethical implications for decision-making and stakeholder trust when visualizations, even unintentionally, violate scientific principles like data integrity. Can a "beautiful lie" be more dangerous than an "ugly truth" in a corporate or analytical context?

Art & Science > The Art

The Art: The Amplifier of Meaning

The "art" is the human layer that transforms a correct chart into a compelling story. This isn't decoration; it's functional art used to guide the user.

Correct, But Lacks Focus

This chart is accurate, but all trends compete for attention. Where is the story?

Artful, Focused, & Insightful

Here, art serves science. A single accent color instantly guides our eye to the key trend.

Discussion: Consider a dashboard you’ve encountered in a professional setting. How effectively did its "artistic" elements (color palette, layout, typography) guide your attention and enhance the narrative? How can strategic aesthetic choices can influence perceived urgency or opportunity in a business context, and how to balance this persuasive power with objective reporting.

Art & Science > The Pitfall

The Pitfall: When the 'Wow' Fades

This happens when art overshadows science. The user spends all their energy trying to read the chart, with no energy left to understand the data.

Cluttered Chart Junk

This chart is overloaded with unnecessary elements: busy background, redundant legend, and distracting patterns.

The Path to Disengagement

1. Beautiful, Complex Chart
↓
2. Confusion: Form Obscures Function
↓
3. Engagement Fades

Discussion: In a competitive business environment, there can be pressure to create "flashy" reports or dashboards that impress stakeholders, even if they sacrifice clarity. How do you, as an information system specialist or business analyst, push back against the creation of "chart junk" when it's requested by a manager or client? What are the long-term costs of prioritizing initial "awe" over sustained "understanding" in data communication?

Art & Science > The Goal

The Goal: Elegant Efficiency

The best visualizations are a seamless synthesis. The "art" is in service of the "science." The beauty isn't the complexity, it's the profound clarity.

"Wow... I get it!"

Discussion: Considering your role in an organization, what are your top 2-3 considerations to achieve "Elegant Efficiency" for your specific audience (e.g., C-suite, operations team, clients)? How do you measure if a visualization has truly led to that "Wow... I get it!" moment versus just a fleeting impression?

Effectiveness Profile > Introduction

The Data Visualization Effectiveness Profile

A Student's Guide to Evaluating Visualizations
(Based on Stephen Few)

Use this guide to critique visualizations for your assignment. Scroll to begin.

Effectiveness Profile > Core Categories

The Two Core Categories

Stephen Few's framework is built on two simple questions. A visualization must be Informative. It can also be Emotive.

1. Informative (The "Must-Have")

Does the visualization produce the right understanding? This category is your primary focus.

  • Usefulness, Completeness, Perceptibility, Truthfulness, Intuitiveness

2. Emotive (The "Nice-to-Have")

Does the visualization produce a useful emotional response? This is about how the chart feels.

  • Aesthetics, Engagement

Effectiveness Profile > Informative (1-2)

Informative: The "Why" & "What"

🎯

1. Usefulness

Useless ‹---› Very Useful

Does this visualization matter to its intended audience? Does it help them make a decision? A perfectly designed chart about useless data is still a failed visualization.

🧩

2. Completeness

No context ‹---› Full context

Does the chart provide all information needed for understanding? Completeness usually requires context, such as a comparison to a target, a historical trend, or an average.

Effectiveness Profile > Informative (3)

Informative: 3. Perceptibility

Is the information easy for the human brain to perceive? This is all about choosing the right chart for the job.

POOR PERCEPTIBILITY

Treemaps & Pie Charts use area, which is hard to compare accurately.

HIGH PERCEPTIBILITY

Bar charts use length on a common baseline, which is clear and precise.

Effectiveness Profile > Informative (4)

Informative: 4. Truthfulness

Is the visualization accurate and valid? A bar chart with a Y-axis that doesn't start at zero is untruthful.

UNTRUTHFUL (Truncated Axis)

This chart makes Jan look tiny and Jun look massive, exaggerating the change.

TRUTHFUL (Axis at Zero)

This is the honest view. The variation is small. A line chart is also a better choice here.

Effectiveness Profile > Informative (5)

Informative: 5. Intuitiveness

🧠

Intuitiveness

Unfamiliar & Difficult ‹---› Familiar & Easy

Is the chart familiar and easy for its specific audience to understand? A bar chart is familiar to almost everyone. A parallel coordinates plot is not.

💡

The Trade-Off

Sometimes, a less intuitive chart is necessary because it's the only way to show complex data (the `Completeness` criterion). This is a trade-off you must justify.

Effectiveness Profile > Emotive (6-7)

Emotive Criteria: The "Feel"

🎨

6. Aesthetics

Ugly ‹---› Pleasing ‹---› Beautiful

A "pleasing" aesthetic is usually achieved by simplicity. Remove clutter, use a clean font, use thoughtful colors, and give the content space to breathe.

🧲

7. Engagement

Distracts from data ‹---› Draws one into data

This is not chart junk. True engagement draws the user into the data. A clean chart that highlights a key insight is highly engaging.

Effectiveness Profile > Assignment Checklist

Assignment Checklist

Use these 7 questions to form your group's evaluation. Remember: Focus on the "Informative" criteria first!

  • 1. Usefulness: Does it have a clear purpose and audience?
  • 2. Completeness: Does it provide necessary context (comparisons, targets)?
  • 3. Perceptibility: Is it the right chart type for the data? (bar vs. pie).
  • 4. Truthfulness: Is it accurate? Does the Y-axis start at 0?
  • 5. Intuitiveness: Is it simple to understand for its intended audience?
  • 6. Aesthetics: Is it clean, uncluttered, and pleasing?
  • 7. Engagement: Does it use design (e.g., color) to highlight key insights?
