The Two Core Categories

Stephen Few's framework is built on two simple questions. A visualization must be Informative. It can also be Emotive.

1. Informative (The "Must-Have")

Does the visualization produce the right understanding? This category is your primary focus. It includes 5 criteria:

  • Usefulness
  • Completeness
  • Perceptibility
  • Truthfulness
  • Intuitiveness

2. Emotive (The "Nice-to-Have")

Does the visualization produce a useful emotional response? This is about how the chart feels and its ability to draw you in. It includes 2 criteria:

  • Aesthetics
  • Engagement

The Trade-Off

Sometimes, a less intuitive chart (like Minard's famous map) is necessary because it's the only way to show complex data (the `Completeness` criterion). In these cases, the chart fails on intuitiveness but may be the most effective choice if the audience is willing to learn how to read it.

Assignment Checklist

Use these 7 questions to form your group's evaluation. Remember: Focus on the "Informative" criteria first! A "pretty" dashboard that is untruthful or unclear is a failed dashboard.

  • 1. Usefulness: Does the visualization have a clear purpose and audience? What decision does it support?
  • 2. Completeness: Does it provide necessary context (like comparisons, targets, or history)?
  • 3. Perceptibility: Is it the right chart type for the data? (e.g., bar vs. pie, line vs. bar).
  • 4. Truthfulness: Is it accurate? Does the Y-axis start at 0? Is it free of 3D distortion?
  • 5. Intuitiveness: Is it simple and easy to understand for its intended audience?
  • 6. Aesthetics: Is it clean, uncluttered, and pleasing? Or is it a "chart junk" nightmare?
  • 7. Engagement: Does it use design (e.g., color) to highlight key insights and draw you into the data? Or does it just distract?
